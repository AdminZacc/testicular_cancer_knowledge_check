Testicular Cancer: What Every Man Should Know

Cancer begins when cells grow out of control. These abnormal cells can form in almost any part of the body ‚Äî including the testicles ‚Äî and can spread elsewhere. Understanding testicular cancer starts with knowing how the testicles function.

Testicles (or testes) are two small, golf ball-sized organs located inside the scrotum beneath the penis. They serve two key functions:

  • Producing male hormones like testosterone
  • Creating sperm cells necessary for reproduction

Sperm forms in seminiferous tubules, matures in the epididymis, and travels through the vas deferens to become part of semen.

The testicles are composed of many cell types, and cancer can develop in almost any of them. Cancers are classified by the cell type they originate from.

Germ Cell Tumors (GCTs)

Over 90% of testicular cancers start here. These include:

  • Seminomas ‚Äì Slow-growing, often in men aged 25‚Äì45
  • Non-seminomas ‚Äì More aggressive, often in younger men

Non-seminomas types:

  • Embryonal Carcinoma: Fast-growing, may raise AFP/HCG
  • Yolk Sac Tumor: Common in children, raises AFP
  • Choriocarcinoma: Rare but aggressive, raises HCG
  • Teratomas: Can appear mature, immature, or mixed

This is a non-invasive form of testicular cancer found only under a microscope. It may never spread, so many doctors opt for "watchful waiting" instead of immediate treatment.

These rare tumors develop in hormone-producing tissue:

  • Leydig Cell Tumors: Often benign, may produce hormones
  • Sertoli Cell Tumors: Usually benign, arise in support cells

Cancers from other organs sometimes spread to the testicles. These are called secondary testicular cancers. Examples include:

  • Lymphoma: Most common secondary testicular cancer
  • Leukemia: Especially in boys with acute leukemia
  • Metastatic cancers: From the lung, prostate, kidney, or skin
